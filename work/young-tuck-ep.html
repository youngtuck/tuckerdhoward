Chapter I The Door
Opening
What It Is

Not polished.
Not strategic.
Simply real.

Young Tuck (EP) represents the first body of work created entirely under the Young Tuck name. Built from stock loops, simple MIDI ideas, and improvised vocal techniques, the project documents a beginner's transition from listener to creator.

There were no collaborators, no producers, no studio infrastructure — only a laptop, accessible software, and a willingness to try. What emerged was less a calculated project than a personal breakthrough: proof that creation was possible.

Chapter II Finding
a Name
Identity

Before the music had a direction,
the identity had to exist.

Early experiments included the alias "GPA85," a reference that began humorously and briefly carried symbolic meaning. Ultimately, it felt misaligned — too conceptual, not personal enough.

"Young Tuck" emerged almost casually. It sounded simple, slightly playful, and distinctly human. Most importantly, it was unique — unclaimed, undefined, and open to interpretation.

Committing to the name marked a shift from experimentation to ownership. The artist now had a voice, and the voice had a name.

Chapter III The
Moment
Context

The goal was not a career.
The goal was to make
something real.

With a background in piano, drums, and voice, the process began as exploration: learning GarageBand, stacking vocals, experimenting with autotune, and discovering how sound could be shaped digitally.

Online creators such as Andrew Huang and CrabLord served as informal teachers, demonstrating that music production could be self-taught and immediate. The EP was released while traveling in New Orleans — adding distance and momentum to the experience. For the first time, the music existed publicly on streaming platforms.

Chapter IV How It
Was Made
Production

Limitations were not obstacles.
They were the texture.

Some tracks were recorded on a consumer USB microphone; others captured through a wired gaming headset plugged directly into a computer. What mattered was completion.

—GarageBand as primary DAW
—Stock loops and MIDI composition
—Consumer USB microphone recording
—Gaming headset as backup input
—Autotune as both tool and aesthetic
—Self-engineered, no formal training
Chapter V Sonic
Character
The Sound

Understanding how songs
are built. One layer at a time.

The EP leans toward accessible pop structures and melodic simplicity. The focus was not on innovation but on learnability — understanding how songs are constructed and how vocals interact with instrumentation.

The sound reflects the early stage of a creator discovering both technical constraints and expressive possibilities simultaneously. Heavy autotune, bright tonal palette, loop-based arrangements. Emphasis on vocal presence above all else.

Chapter VI Visual
Identity
The Cover

Photographed in a parking lot.
Symbolic in every way.

Shot outside a knee surgery clinic after a physical therapy session, the cover image captures an unplanned but resonant setting: recovery, transition, forward movement.

Processed in Photoshop — black-and-white portraiture, bright emphasized blue eyes, cursive typography, high contrast, emotional directness. The result is understated but memorable. A first visual declaration of the Young Tuck persona.

"Stutter became something people actually knew. That was the first time music stopped being mine — and started being ours."

Standout track — Stutter
Legacy

Every subsequent project
traces back to this origin.

While later releases would evolve significantly in production quality, conceptual depth, and artistic ambition, this EP remains foundational. It established the Young Tuck identity, a willingness to self-produce, comfort with digital creation tools, the practice of finishing and releasing work, and a lifelong creative trajectory.

Every journey has a first footprint. This was the one that stayed.
